Waste & Containment

Nuclear Waste is considered one of, if not the largest risk of operating nuclear power plants. Solutions seem rarely discussed, and even if they are, many are effective only in the short-term.

Recent research has produced viable concepts for solving "the waste problem" going forward. A notable example comes from the Journal of Nuclear Materials, which published an abstract on the storage of nuclear waste within blast-furnace slag--this material, compromised of unused ceramics and metals,is said to reduce of volume of the waste by anywhere between eighty and ninety five percent (Journal of Nuclear Materials, Volume 444, 2014)

Power Generation

11% of the world's energy is produced by nuclear means, stemming from hundreds of reactors all over the globe (Nuclear Power in the World Today, 2018). Nuclear power is the second largest source of low-CO2 energy generation globally, surpassed by only hydroelectricy.

As low-carbon solutions are going to have to expand dramatically in the next few decades (to counter the phasing of fossil fuel based solutions), nuclear is positioned as a land-dense, proven alternative. As such, it is expected to expand to 17% of all energy generation by 2060 (World Energy Needs and Nuclear Power, 2017)

Conclusion

With growing demand and increased need for low-carbon energy production, a nuclear option contains the potential to bridge the gap between renewables of the future and the fossils fuels of the past and present. Technology, such as the use of waste slag to contain radioactive particles, helps to minimize what is the greatest obstacle to nuclear power today--waste. Together, demand and technology ought to drive the human race to strive for creative, innovative and efficient solutions to our energy crisis. Nuclear power could just well be the answer we need--staring in our faces the entire time.

Additional Information

-Many 'Advanced Fourth Generation' reactors can utilize old radioactive byproducts in what is called a "burner" fuel cycle, thus rendering them less dangerous and often not at all. (Waste Burner)

-Virtrification (conversion of waste into solids) is already being used by Russia, France and more in order to reduce the danger and volume of nuclear byproducts (Treatment and Conditioning of Nuclear Waste)
